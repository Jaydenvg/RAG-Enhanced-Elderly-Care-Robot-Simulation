# COPD (Chronic Obstructive Pulmonary Disease) Medications

## BRONCHODILATORS - SHORT-ACTING

### What Are They?
Open airways in lungs for quick relief.

### Common Types
- Albuterol (Ventolin, ProAir)
- Levalbuterol (Xopenex)

### How to Use (Inhaler)
1. Shake inhaler
2. Breathe out completely
3. Put mouth around mouthpiece
4. Breathe in while pressing down
5. Hold breath 10 seconds
6. Breathe out slowly

### When to Use
- When feeling short of breath
- Before activity or exercise
- As needed, up to 4-6 times daily

### Side Effects
- Shaking (normal)
- Nervousness
- Headache
- Usually temporary

---

## BRONCHODILATORS - LONG-ACTING

### What Are They?
Prevent symptoms by keeping airways open all day.

### Common Types
- Salmeterol (long-acting beta-agonist)
- Tiotropium (anticholinergic, very popular)

### How to Use
- Once or twice daily as prescribed
- Take consistently
- Don't skip doses

### Benefits
- Fewer attacks if taken regularly
- Better breathing throughout day
- Improved sleep

---

## INHALED CORTICOSTEROIDS

### What Are They?
Reduce inflammation in lungs.

### Common Types
- Fluticasone
- Budesonide
- Beclomethasone

### How to Use
- Usually twice daily
- Regular use needed for benefit
- Don't stop suddenly

### Important
- Rinse mouth after use (prevents thrush)
- Different from oral steroids

---

## OXYGEN THERAPY

### When Needed
- Blood oxygen too low
- Severe COPD
- Exertion causing low oxygen

### Types
- **Stationary:** Home machine for rest
- **Portable:** Tanks for mobility
- **Liquid:** Lighter weight

### How to Use
- Nasal cannula or face mask
- Follow oxygen prescription exactly
- Don't adjust flow yourself

### Safety
- Oxygen supports fire - keep away from flames
- Don't smoke when using oxygen
- Proper storage important

---

## EMERGENCY MEDICATIONS

### Rescue Inhaler (Albuterol)
- Use when having difficulty breathing
- Helps quickly open airways
- Always carry with you

### When to Use Emergency Medications
- Increased shortness of breath
- Yellow or green mucus cough
- Fever with respiratory symptoms
- Chest pain

### When to Call Doctor
- No improvement with rescue inhaler
- Using rescue more than 2 times weekly
- Severe shortness of breath
- Coughing up blood

### When to Go to ER/911
- Unable to speak full sentences
- Extreme difficulty breathing
- Confusion or loss of consciousness
- Severe chest pain

---

## MANAGING FLARE-UPS

### What Is a Flare-Up?
- Sudden worsening of symptoms
- May need hospitalization
- Usually triggered by infection

### Triggers
- Respiratory infections
- Air pollution
- Cold weather
- Stress
- Allergens

### Treatment
- More frequent use of rescue inhaler
- Oral steroids (if prescribed)
- Antibiotics (if infection)
- Rest and fluids
- Call doctor for guidance

---

## INHALER TECHNIQUE - CRITICAL!

### Proper Technique Is Essential
- Improper use = medication doesn't work
- Doctor or respiratory therapist should show you
- Practice in front of mirror

### Common Mistakes
- Not shaking inhaler
- Wrong breathing technique
- Not holding breath long enough
- Not rinsing mouth after use

### Ask Doctor If Unsure
- Demonstration important
- Video tutorials available
- Spacer devices help (ask doctor)

---

## LIFESTYLE WITH COPD

### Avoid Triggers
- No smoking or secondhand smoke
- Avoid air pollution
- Avoid respiratory infections
- Avoid allergens when possible

### Exercise
- Pulmonary rehabilitation programs
- Gentle exercise improves function
- Walking often recommended

### Vaccinations
- Flu shot yearly
- Pneumonia vaccine
- COVID-19 vaccine
- Pertussis vaccine (if not previous)

### Nutrition
- Stay well-nourished
- Small meals easier if short of breath
- Stay hydrated

---

## KEY POINTS
- Take medications consistently as prescribed
- Proper inhaler technique critical
- Know emergency warning signs
- Avoid triggers when possible
- Regular monitoring by doctor important
- Vaccinations important
