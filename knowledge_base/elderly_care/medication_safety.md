# Medication Safety for Elderly Patients

## Why Medication Safety Matters
Elderly patients often take multiple medications (polypharmacy).
Each additional medication increases risk of:
- Side effects
- Drug interactions
- Accidental overdose
- Confusion about dosing

## The Golden Rules

### Rule 1: Take Exactly as Prescribed
- Same time every day if possible
- Exact dose (don't skip or take extra)
- Same number of days (don't stop early)
- Even if you feel fine

### Rule 2: Tell Your Doctor Everything
- All prescription medications
- All over-the-counter medications
- All supplements and vitamins
- All herbal products
- Alcohol consumption
- Allergies and reactions

### Rule 3: One Pharmacy
- Use same pharmacy for all prescriptions
- Pharmacist can catch interactions
- Better record of all medications
- Can answer questions

### Rule 4: Keep a Medication List
Keep written list including:
- Medication name
- Dose
- When you take it (morning/afternoon/evening)
- Reason you take it
- Side effects you experience
- Show list to all doctors

### Rule 5: Don't Share Medications
- Never give your pills to someone else
- Never take someone else's medications
- Prescribed for YOU specifically

## Common Medication Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Forgetting to Take Medication
- Use pill organizer (labeled by day)
- Set phone alarms for medication times
- Connect to daily routine (after breakfast, before bed)
- Ask family member to remind you

### Mistake 2: Mixing Up Medications
- Keep medications in original labeled bottles
- Don't mix multiple medications in same bottle
- Use pill organizer for each day
- Compare bottle label with organizer

### Mistake 3: Stopping Medications Too Soon
- Don't stop because you feel better
- Blood pressure meds work because you take them
- Stopping suddenly can be dangerous
- Always ask doctor before stopping

### Mistake 4: Taking Extra Doses
- Don't take double dose if you forgot
- Skip missed dose if close to next time
- Extra medication = overdose risk
- Call pharmacist if unsure

### Mistake 5: Not Reporting Side Effects
- Side effects may be serious
- Doctor may have alternative
- Some side effects go away with time
- Others need medication change

## Storage and Handling

### Proper Storage
- Keep in original labeled bottle
- Store in cool, dry place
- NOT in bathroom (heat/moisture)
- NOT in kitchen (heat)
- Keep away from children
- Keep away from pets
- Check expiration dates

### Disposal
- Don't flush down toilet (except specific meds)
- Don't throw in trash
- Return to pharmacy for disposal
- Many pharmacies have take-back programs
- Safely dispose of old medications

## Understanding Medication Labels

**Example:** "Take 1 tablet by mouth twice daily with food"
- **1 tablet** = amount
- **by mouth** = swallow (don't chew)
- **twice daily** = morning and evening
- **with food** = take with a meal

## Interactions to Watch

### Common Problem: NSAIDs + Blood Pressure Meds
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) reduce effectiveness
- Can harm kidneys
- Use acetaminophen instead for pain

### Common Problem: Alcohol + Blood Pressure Meds
- Increases dizziness
- Risk of fainting
- Limit alcohol (ideally none)

### Common Problem: Multiple Medications + Dizziness
- Many meds can cause dizziness
- Increased fall risk
- Move slowly when getting up
- Hold onto handrails

## When to Call Doctor
- New side effect
- Medication not working
- Can't remember how to take it
- Feeling confused about doses
- Concerned about interactions
- Want to stop medication
- Allergic reaction

## Emergency: Overdose Signs
- Severe drowsiness
- Confusion or difficulty thinking
- Difficulty breathing
- Loss of consciousness
- Severe chest pain
- Seizures

**If overdose suspected: Call 911 or poison control immediately**

## Key Points
Medications only work if taken correctly.
More medications = higher risk of problems.
Communication with doctor and pharmacist is essential.
Ask questions - there are no stupid questions about medications.
Your safety is the priority.
